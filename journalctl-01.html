Find Failure in the Journal

This lesson is a small practical guide to service troubleshooting with systemctl and journalctl. The goal is not only to pass the mission, but to understand how an administrator narrows a failing service from "it is down" to one precise startup problem.

Mission Goal

  • Confirm that the service is really failing
  • Use the journal to find the exact startup complaint
  • Repair only the missing dependency the service actually needs
  • Restart and prove the service is healthy again

What Layer This Is

This mission lives in the service-management and operating-system troubleshooting layer. The application itself is not your first concern here. Your first concern is the service lifecycle around it:

  • Did the unit start or fail?
  • What does the service manager know about the failure?
  • What does the service itself say in its logs?
  • Is the missing piece a path, directory, permission, config value, or dependency?

That is classic sysadmin work. Before changing application code, you prove whether the failure is actually in the runtime environment around the service.

What We Are Using and Why

systemctl and journalctl are two of the most important day-to-day tools on a Linux system that uses systemd.

  • systemctl is used to inspect and control services
  • journalctl is used to read the service journal and see what the service reported during startup or failure

An administrator uses them together because they answer different questions:

  • systemctl status answers: "Is this unit active, failed, or inactive?"
  • journalctl -u ... answers: "What exactly happened when it tried to start?"

How It Works in Real Operations

When a service fails, the service manager often shows only the state and a limited failure summary. That is useful, but not always enough. The journal is where you usually find the higher-signal clue: the exact file, path, directory, dependency, or permission that blocked startup.

This is why experienced administrators usually do not jump straight to restart loops. A better order is:

  1. Confirm the unit is actually failed
  2. Read the recent journal lines for that unit
  3. Identify the exact complaint
  4. Verify the complaint on disk or in config
  5. Repair only that layer
  6. Restart and validate

How To Use It in Practice

A strong admin workflow here is:

  • Start by confirming that the unit is actually failing, not only "reported broken" by a teammate
  • Read the recent journal lines for that unit and look for the most concrete startup complaint
  • Take the path, directory, or dependency mentioned in the log and verify whether it really exists on disk
  • Repair only the missing dependency the service is complaining about
  • Restart the unit and check whether its state changed from failed to active
  • Run a final service or health validation so you prove the service is usable, not only restarted

What matters is the reasoning: confirm failure, read the service's own words, verify the missing piece, repair that exact layer, then validate the result.

What Good Evidence Looks Like

  • The unit state is clearly failed before you attempt a fix
  • The journal shows a concrete startup complaint, not only a generic failure state
  • The complaint points to a real missing path or dependency you can verify on disk
  • The service becomes active only after that exact dependency is restored

What Not To Do

  • Do not restart the unit repeatedly before reading the journal
  • Do not create random directories just because the service failed
  • Do not edit unrelated config or binaries before proving what the service is missing

Common Problems Administrators See

  • A cleanup job removes a runtime directory the service expects
  • A service fails because a mount, path, or socket does not exist
  • The operator keeps restarting the unit without reading the journal
  • The service manager only says "failed", while the journal contains the real root cause

In practice, many outages look "application-like" at first, but are actually environment problems around the service.

What You Should Learn From This Mission

The real lesson is not only "use journalctl". The real lesson is to troubleshoot in layers:

  • service state first
  • service logs second
  • filesystem or config verification third
  • repair only after the evidence is clear

This is the habit that keeps you from making random fixes under pressure.
